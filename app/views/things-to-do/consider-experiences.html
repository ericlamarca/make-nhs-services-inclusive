Consider the experiences of groups who are often excluded

Contributors

Ask yourself who could be excluded by your service

Some groups of people, particularly minority groups, are more likely to be excluded than others. This can be because of their age, abilities, ethnicity, a lack of resources or other circumstances.

Example

If someone who is pregnant does not identify as a woman, they may feel excluded by breastfeeding content. We interviewed trans men who had given birth and are creating new content on chestfeeding for this audience.

Do not make assumptions about your users

Be aware of your own biases and privilege, so that your design doesn’t just reflect your own lived experiences.

Carry out user research to make sure your design assumptions are correct.

Example

Some of our pregnancy content assumed that people were happy about their pregnancy. But a pregnant 15-year-old may not have the same needs as someone who's older with a planned pregnancy. So we considered the needs of people with different feelings.

Understand the dynamics of a group

It’s important to understand the social and cultural dynamics of a group. If you’re going to do research with certain people, maybe ask yourself questions like:

  • Should I be the one doing this research? Will my presence be detrimental to the process?
  • Should we be speaking to people in smaller groups? Are there power dynamics between people that we need to be aware of?
  • Do you know enough about the history of the group? Are you aware of topics, areas or past events that might impact what you want to talk about?

Understand how to get the best engagement out of a group

It is important to bear things like literacy and numeracy levels and trust in mind when you are talking to or designing for a certain group. Be prepared to adapt your approaches to get the best engagement out of a group of people.

You should always be trying to adapt yourself and your methodologies first, rather than trying to force people to adapt to you. Be flexible, and prepared not to finish everything you wanted.

Use cognitive walkthroughs

A cognitive walkthrough is a tool used to evaluate the usability of a service. It works by using a series of tasks and questions, looking at things from a certain user’s perspective.

Example

[A team] used cognitive walkthroughs when building an image gallery. It helped them identify a potential problem for speech-to-text users which they hadn’t identified before.

As a result of the cognitive walkthrough, they labelled each thumbnail in the gallery with a number. They ensured that it was coded in a way that would allow speech-to-text users to easily instruct their assistive tech to click on them. [link to a small case study?].
